Pergola Deep Dive

10 x 12 ft, freestanding, backyard

Cost Summary

DIY from scratch: $585-$1,700 (pressure-treated to cedar)
DIY from kit: $800-$2,500 depending on material and features
Contractor installed: $3,000-$9,000 (basic to custom)

DIY vs. Contractor

DIY is realistic if...

  • You have built a deck, done framing, or assembled large structures before
  • You have a reliable helper (beams are heavy, 8-10 ft up)
  • You are building freestanding (not attached to house)
  • You own or can rent: post-hole digger/auger, circular saw, drill, 4-ft level

Time commitment: 2-3 days with a helper. Day 1: layout, digging, setting posts. Wait 24-48 hrs for concrete to cure. Day 2-3: beams, rafters, finishing. A kit saves ~40% of build time.

Hire a contractor if...

  • You want it attached to the house (ledger board flashing done wrong causes water damage)
  • Your local code requires engineered drawings or inspections
  • You want a louvered or motorized roof (electrical + precision)
  • Ground is rocky, heavily sloped, or has underground utilities

Permits

A 10x12 is exactly 120 sq ft, right on the common exemption boundary. Some jurisdictions say "under 120" (you need a permit), others say "120 or less" (you don't). Call your local building/zoning dept to confirm. 5-minute call saves thousands in fines.

What triggers a permit:

  • Attached to house (almost always requires permit)
  • Solid roof (polycarbonate, metal, louvers may reclassify as "patio cover")
  • Electrical added (outlets, fans, lighting)
  • Setback violations (too close to property line, typically 3-10 ft)
  • HOA has its own approval process regardless of municipal rules

Permit fee if needed: ~$50-$150. Approval typically 1-3 weeks.

Tips and Gotchas

Call 811 before you dig. Free utility locating. Non-negotiable. Hitting a gas or electrical line is life-threatening.

Before You Start

  • Check your property survey. Know your property lines. Setback violations can force removal.
  • Plan for furniture first. A 10x12 fits a 6-person dining set comfortably, but barely. Allow 3 ft clearance around seating.

During the Build

  • Square your layout using the 3-4-5 triangle method. Measure diagonals (must be equal). 1 inch off at the footings = 3 inches off at the top.
  • Post depth: minimum 24". In cold climates, go below frost line (36-48" in northern states).
  • Use post saddles/brackets on concrete footings. Do NOT bury wood directly in concrete. The wood-concrete interface traps moisture and accelerates rot.
  • Pre-drill everything. Cedar and PT lumber split easily near ends.
  • Brace posts temporarily with 2x4 diagonal braces staked to the ground.

Common Mistakes

  • Undersized posts. 4x4 is too flimsy for 10x12. Use 6x6 minimum.
  • Ignoring slope. If yard slopes 6" across 12 ft, posts need different lengths. Measure all four corners.
  • Skipping the stain/seal. Even cedar turns gray and checks within one season unfinished.
  • Buying wet/green lumber. PT wood from big-box stores is often soaking wet. Let it acclimate or buy kiln-dried (KDAT).
  • Forgetting drainage. If you add a solid roof later, plan for gutters or slope rafters 1/4" per foot.
  • Ignoring wind loads. A canopy or solid roof catches wind like a sail. Use engineered connectors (Simpson Strong-Tie), not toenailed screws.
Pro tips from people who've done it: Stain all lumber before assembly (you'll never reach the tops of beams later). Run conduit or low-voltage wire through posts during construction for lighting/fans. The "real" cost of DIY is time, not money: you save $1,500-$3,000 in labor but spend 2-3 weekends.
